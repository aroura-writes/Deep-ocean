Deep Ocean & Night Sky

Where stars fall
into the sea

On bioluminescence, depth, and the living light below

Look up on a clear night and you see a sky scattered with light. Look beneath the ocean's surface after dark, and you find something that mirrors it — not a reflection, but a living answer. The deep ocean glows from within.

The parallel worlds

A sky above, a sky below

The night sky and the deep ocean share the same quality of darkness broken by scattered points of light. But while starlight travels millions of kilometres to reach us, ocean light is made right here — produced by living organisms through a process called bioluminescence.

Scientists estimate that more than 75% of deep-sea organisms are capable of producing their own light. In terms of sheer number of light-producing species, the ocean's depths rival the number of stars visible to the naked eye.

The science

How living things make light

Bioluminescence is a chemical reaction. An organic compound called luciferin reacts with oxygen, catalysed by an enzyme called luciferase. This reaction releases energy not as heat but as visible light — almost always blue or blue-green, the wavelengths that travel furthest through seawater.

Key facts
01The reaction is nearly 100% energy-efficient — almost no heat is wasted. A light bulb, by comparison, loses 90% of its energy as heat.
02Most bioluminescent light is blue (440–480 nm) because red wavelengths are absorbed by water within a few metres of the surface.
03Below 200 metres, sunlight essentially disappears. Bioluminescence is the only light source in this zone.

Living lanterns

Creatures of the glowing deep

Each species uses its light for a different purpose — to lure prey, to warn predators, to find a mate, or to communicate.

Anglerfish
Uses a bioluminescent lure dangling above its mouth to attract prey in total darkness. The light is produced by symbiotic bacteria inside the lure.
Dinoflagellates
Microscopic plankton responsible for the "blue wave" effect on coastlines at night. Mechanical disturbance — such as a wave — triggers their flash.
Firefly squid
Found off Japan, this small squid has thousands of photophores across its body, producing a bright blue glow used for camouflage and communication.
Vampire squid
Despite its name, it feeds on marine snow. It can flash every photophore simultaneously — a defensive dazzle to confuse predators.

Light by depth

How common is bioluminescence at each depth?

As sunlight fades with depth, bioluminescence becomes the dominant form of light. The deeper you go, the more species depend on it.

0 – 200 m (sunlit)
~30%
200 – 1,000 m (twilight)
~60%
1,000 – 4,000 m (midnight)
~80%
4,000 m+ (abyss)

Why it matters

The ocean's light as a mirror of the sky

The parallel between starlight and bioluminescence is not merely poetic. Both represent the emission of photons from energy-releasing reactions — nuclear fusion in stars, chemical oxidation in marine organisms. The universe at every scale finds ways to turn energy into light.

We have explored less than 20% of the ocean floor. The living lights we have not yet seen may outnumber those we have.
